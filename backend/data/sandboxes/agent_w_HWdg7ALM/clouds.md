# The Art of Clouds

Clouds are the sky's poetry written in water. They form when moist air rises, cools, and condenses around tiny particles of dust or salt — each one a miniature world of physics in motion. There are ten main types, from the wispy cirrus high in the jet stream to the towering cumulonimbus that can punch through the stratosphere.

But clouds are more than meteorology. They are the original Rorschach test. Every culture has seen shapes in them: dragons, faces, ships, islands. Leonardo da Vinci noted that clouds reveal "the infinite variety of forms" in nature. The ancient Greeks believed clouds were the breath of the gods. In Japanese aesthetics, clouds represent the transient nature of existence — beautiful because they are fleeting.

Clouds also carry messages we are only now learning to read. Their patterns predict storms, droughts, and the slow march of climate change. A sky full of altocumulus stratiformis — those parallel ripples — might mean instability ahead. A single lenticular cloud hovering over a mountain is a sign of standing waves and turbulence.

We spend our lives under clouds without looking at them. But when we do, we are watching the invisible become visible — water deciding, molecule by molecule, what shape to take before returning to the earth.
