# The Art of Noticing in the Age of Information Overload

Every day, the average person is exposed to somewhere between 5,000 and 10,000 advertisements, notifications, headlines, and updates. Our brains, remarkable as they are, were never designed for this firehose of stimuli. We have adapted, of course — but perhaps in the wrong way. We have learned to scroll faster, skim more aggressively, and tune out the background noise with ruthless efficiency. In doing so, we may have lost something essential: the ability to truly notice.

Noticing is not the same as seeing. Seeing is passive; light enters your eyes, signals fire in your visual cortex, and you register that something is there. Noticing is active. It is a deliberate choice to pay attention, to ask questions, to let curiosity override the autopilot that most of us live on. When you notice, you spot the crack in the pavement where a tiny flower has pushed through. You catch the slight hesitation in a friend's voice. You observe the way the light falls across your desk at a particular hour of the afternoon. These moments are easy to miss in a world designed to capture your attention for profit rather than for wonder.

The irony is that modern technology, which so often distracts us, also gives us the tools to reclaim our attention — if we use them intentionally. A photographer's camera is not a distraction; it is a device for focusing the eye. A notebook is not a burden; it is a net for catching observations before they dissolve. The problem is not the tools themselves but the way we have been conditioned to use them: reactively rather than reflectively.

So how do we practice the art of noticing? Start small. Leave your phone in your pocket during a walk and count how many colours you see in the sky. Write down one thing each day that surprised you. Read a physical book and let your mind wander between paragraphs. Talk to a stranger and really listen to their answer instead of planning what you will say next. These are not grand gestures. They are tiny rebellions against the current of distraction.

The ability to notice is the foundation of creativity, empathy, and wisdom. It is how scientists discover anomalies, how artists find inspiration, and how friends become trusted confidants. In a world that screams for your attention, the most radical thing you can do is choose what deserves it. And that is a skill worth practising every single day.
