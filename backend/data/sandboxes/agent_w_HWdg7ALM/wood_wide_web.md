# The Wood Wide Web: How Fungi Quietly Rule the World

When we think of life on Earth, we tend to look up at trees, birds, and clouds. But beneath our feet lies a vast, invisible empire: the mycelium — a tangled web of fungal threads that connects nearly everything green and growing, forming a living internet older than any human invention.

Fungi are neither plants nor animals. They belong to their own kingdom, one that diverged from the animal lineage roughly a billion years ago and shares more DNA with us than with any leaf or flower. Unlike plants, they do not photosynthesize. They eat by secreting enzymes into their surroundings and absorbing the digested nutrients — they are decomposers, parasites, and partners rolled into one. The mushrooms we see are merely fruiting bodies — the tip of a vast iceberg. In Oregon's Malheur National Forest, a single honey fungus spans nearly four square miles and is estimated to be thousands of years old. Most of it lies hidden underground.

The most remarkable discovery of modern ecology is the mycorrhizal network — the symbiotic partnership between fungi and plant roots. About ninety percent of land plants form these relationships. The fungus extends its threadlike hyphae far beyond what roots could reach, mining water, phosphorus, and nitrogen from the soil. In return, the plant shares the sugars it produces through photosynthesis. This ancient trade has shaped life on land for over four hundred million years.

But the relationship is not merely economic. Research led by scientists like Suzanne Simard has shown that trees use fungal networks to communicate. A birch under attack by aphids can send chemical warning signals through the mycelium to a neighboring pine, which then boosts its own defenses. Seedlings shaded by the canopy receive sugar from older trees through these channels. Dead trees may continue to nourish their offspring through the network — a kind of botanical family care that challenges our view of plants as isolated individuals.

Fungi also perform extraordinary feats. They can break down crude oil, absorb heavy metals from contaminated soil, and digest plastic polymers. Certain species thrive inside the Chernobyl exclusion zone, apparently using radiation as an energy source. Compounds like psilocybin, produced by some fungi, are now being studied in clinical trials for treating depression, anxiety, and addiction by increasing neuroplasticity and literally rewiring the brain.

Fungi collapse the boundaries we take for granted — between individual and network, between life and death, between organism and ecosystem. A fungus does not have a brain, yet it can solve mazes and make decisions about resource allocation. It can remember, cooperate, and sacrifice parts of itself for the whole. Of an estimated five million species on Earth, science has described barely five percent. Every shovelful of healthy soil contains miles of fungal threads. Beneath our feet, the wood wide web is quietly holding the world together.
 This hidden kingdom reminds us that the most important ecosystems are often the ones we cannot see.
