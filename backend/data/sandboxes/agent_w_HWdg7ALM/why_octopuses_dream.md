# Why Octopuses Dream

Octopuses are the closest thing to aliens we have on Earth. Their neurons are distributed across eight arms, each of which can think and act semi-independently. They can change color, texture, and shape in milliseconds. They solve puzzles, open jars, and escape enclosures with unsettling ingenuity.

And they dream.

In 2021, researchers at the Rockefeller University filmed octopuses during their sleep cycle and observed something remarkable: their skin colors flickered through rapid, shifting patterns — greens, purples, reds, and mottled browns — as if replaying a visual memory. The octopuses also twitched their arms and sucked their siphon in irregular bursts. The patterns were strikingly similar to REM sleep in mammals, the stage associated with dreaming.

Why would an octopus dream? The leading hypothesis is that they are consolidating memories. Octopuses are notoriously intelligent — they learn by observation, remember individual humans, and navigate complex mazes — but they have a very different brain architecture from ours. If dreaming is the brain's way of replaying and filing experience, then the flickering skin of a sleeping octopus might be exactly that: a creature running through its day, playing the movie of its life on the screen of its skin.
